Foundation of Economics

-Meaning of Economic Science:
Economics is the study of how people use limited resources to satisfy unlimited wants.
It studies production, distribution and consumption of goods and services.

-Nature of Economic Science:
1. Science of Wealth – Studies how wealth is earned and used.
2. Science of Human Behaviour – Studies how people make economic decisions.
3. Social Science – Studies economic activities in society.
4. Deals with Scarcity – Resources are limited but wants are unlimited.
5. Both Positive and Normative – Explains facts and suggests policies.

-Significance of Economic Science:
1. Helps in proper use of limited resources.
2. Helps government in policy making.
3. Helps in solving problems like poverty, unemployment, inflation.
4. Helps in business decision making.
5. Improves standard of living.

-Relevance of Economics to Law:
1. Tax Laws – Based on income and wealth distribution.
2. Labour Laws – Related to wages and employment.
3. Competition Law – Prevents monopoly and unfair trade practices.
4. Consumer Protection Law – Protects consumers from exploitation.
5. Contract Law – Based on exchange of goods and services.
6. Company Law – Regulates business organisations.

-2. Difference between Micro Economics and Macro Economics

-Micro Economics:
1. Studies individual units (consumer, firm, industry).
2. Studies price of a particular good.
3. Deals with individual demand and supply.
4. Example: Price of sugar, wages in a firm.

-Macro Economics:
1. Studies whole economy.
2. Studies national income, inflation, unemployment.
3. Deals with aggregate demand and supply.
4. Example: Total income of a country, overall price level.

-Main Differences:
1. Meaning: Micro = Small part; Macro = Whole economy.
2. Scope: Micro is narrow; Macro is wide.
3. Focus: Micro focuses on individuals; Macro focuses on aggregates.
4. Examples: Micro – price of rice; Macro – national income.

-3. Positive Economics and Normative Economics

-Positive Economics:
1. Based on facts and data.
2. Explains “what is”.
3. Can be tested and verified.
4. Example: Inflation rate is 6%.

-Normative Economics:
1. Based on opinions and value judgments.
2. Explains “what should be”.
3. Cannot be tested scientifically.
4. Example: Government should control inflation.

-Main Difference:
1. Positive = Fact based.
2. Normative = Opinion based.
3. Positive describes reality.
4. Normative suggests policies.

2. Law of demand, Elasticity of Demand and Law of supply


-1. Law of Demand

-Meaning:
The Law of Demand states that, other things remaining constant (ceteris paribus), when the price of a good increases, its quantity demanded decreases, and when the price decreases, quantity demanded increases.

-Definition:
There is an inverse relationship between price and quantity demanded.

Example:
If the price of mangoes is ₹100 per kg, a consumer buys 1 kg.
If the price falls to ₹50 per kg, the consumer buys 3 kg.
Thus, price ↓ = demand ↑ and price ↑ = demand ↓.

-Assumptions:
1. Income of consumer remains constant.
2. Taste and preferences remain same.
3. Prices of related goods remain constant.
4. No change in population.
5. No expectation of future price change.

-Exceptions:
1. Giffen goods (e.g., very poor consumers buying more cheap rice even when price rises).
2. Luxury goods (e.g., branded watches – higher price may increase demand).
3. Fear of shortage (people buy more even if price rises).

-2. Elasticity of Demand

-Meaning:
Elasticity of Demand measures the degree of responsiveness of quantity demanded to change in price.
example:👉 How much people change their buying when the price changes.

-Definition:
It shows how much quantity demanded changes due to change in price.

-Formula:
Elasticity of Demand (Ed) = Percentage change in Quantity Demanded / Percentage change in Price

Example 1 (Elastic Demand):
Price of pizza falls from ₹200 to ₹150 (25% decrease).
Demand increases from 100 units to 150 units (50% increase).
Since demand changes more than price, demand is elastic.

-Factors Affecting Elasticity of Demand:
1. Availability of substitutes.
2. Nature of commodity (necessity or luxury).
3. Income level of consumer.
4. Time period.
5. Number of uses of commodity.

-3. Law of Supply

-Meaning:
The Law of Supply states that, other things remaining constant, when the price of a good increases, its quantity supplied increases, and when the price decreases, quantity supplied decreases.

-Definition:
There is a direct relationship between price and quantity supplied.

Example:
If the price of tomatoes is ₹20 per kg, a farmer supplies 100 kg.
If the price increases to ₹40 per kg, the farmer supplies 200 kg.
Thus, price ↑ = supply ↑ and price ↓ = supply ↓.

-Assumptions:
1. Cost of production remains constant.
2. Technology remains same.
3. No change in government policy.
4. Prices of other goods remain constant.

-Exceptions:
1. Perishable goods (seller may sell at low price to avoid loss).
2. Agricultural goods (production depends on weather).
3. Backward bending supply of labour (workers may work less at very high wages).

3. Market structure- Features of Perfect Competition, Monopoly, Monopolistic Competition and Oligopoly


-Meaning of Market Structure:
Market structure refers to the nature and degree of competition in a market. It depends on number of firms, type of product, entry barriers and control over price.

-1. Perfect Competition

-Meaning:
A market where there are many buyers and sellers selling homogeneous (identical) products and no single seller can control the price.

-Features:
1. Large number of buyers and sellers.
2. Homogeneous product (identical goods).
3. Free entry and exit of firms.
4. Perfect knowledge of market conditions.
5. No control over price (firms are price takers).
6. Perfect mobility of factors of production.

Example:
Agricultural products market like wheat or rice market.

-2. Monopoly

-Meaning:
A market where there is only one seller and many buyers, and the seller has full control over price.

-Features:
1. Single seller.
2. No close substitutes for the product.
3. High barriers to entry.
4. Price maker (firm decides price).
5. Possibility of super normal profit.

Example:
Indian Railways (government monopoly in rail transport in many areas).

-3. Monopolistic Competition

-Meaning:
A market where there are many sellers selling differentiated products.

-Features:
1. Large number of sellers.
2. Product differentiation (brand, quality, design).
3. Free entry and exit.
4. Selling costs (advertisement, branding).
5. Some control over price.

Example:
Toothpaste market (Colgate, Pepsodent, Close-up etc.).

-4. Oligopoly

-Meaning:
A market where there are few large firms dominating the market.

-Features:
1. Few large sellers.
2. Interdependence (decision of one firm affects others).
3. High barriers to entry.
4. Non-price competition (advertisement, offers).
5. Price rigidity (prices remain stable for long time).

Example:
Telecom industry (few companies dominating the market).

-Summary Table:
Perfect Competition → Many firms + identical product + price taker.
Monopoly → One firm + no substitute + price maker.
Monopolistic Competition → Many firms + differentiated product.
Oligopoly → Few firms + interdependence + high competition.

4. Circular Flow of Income


-Meaning:
Circular Flow of Income refers to the continuous flow of money and goods between different sectors of the economy.
It shows how income is generated and circulated in an economy.

-1. Two-Sector Model (Households and Firms)

-Explanation:
This model includes only two sectors:
1.Households
2.Firms

-Flow of Goods and Services:
• Households provide factors of production (land, labour, capital, enterprise) to firms.
• Firms produce goods and services and supply them to households.

1. Ramesh works in a factory.
2. The factory pays Ramesh ₹10,000 salary. → (Income to Ramesh)
3. Ramesh uses this ₹10,000 to buy clothes and food from shops. → (Spending)
4. The shopkeeper now gets ₹10,000.
5. The shopkeeper buys goods from factory.
6. The factory again earns money and pays salary.

-Flow of Money:
• Firms pay wages, rent, interest and profit to households (factor income).
• Households spend money on goods and services produced by firms (consumption expenditure).

Thus, income flows in a circular manner between households and firms.

-2. Three-Sector Model (Including Government)

-Explanation:
This model includes:
1. Households
2. Firms
3. Government

-Role of Government:
• Government collects taxes from households and firms.
• Government provides public goods and services.
• Government gives subsidies and transfers.

Example:
A worker earns ₹30,000 and pays ₹3,000 as tax to the government.
The government uses this tax money to build roads and schools.
This spending creates jobs and income for others.
Thus, income keeps circulating with government involvement.

-3. Four-Sector Model (Open Economy)

-Explanation:
This model includes:
1. Households
2. Firms
3. Government
4. Foreign Sector (Rest of the World)

-Foreign Sector Activities:
• Exports – Goods sold to other countries (inflow of money).
• Imports – Goods purchased from other countries (outflow of money).

- example:Households give labor to Firms and get wages
Households spend money on goods (Consumption - C)
Firms produce goods and pay wages
Government collects taxes (T) and gives spending (G)
Foreign sector involves exports (X) and imports (M)

5. National Income and its measurement (GDP, NDP,GNP, NNP, PCI, GVA, Green GDP)


-National Income and Its Measurement

-Meaning:
National Income is the total money earned by a country from all goods and services produced in one year.
It tells us how rich or developed a country’s economy is.

-Importance:
1. Shows economic growth.
2. Helps government plan policies.
3. Tells standard of living.
4. Useful for comparing with other countries.

-Ways to Measure National Income:
1. Income Method: Add all incomes people earn (wages, rent, profit, interest).
2. Expenditure Method: Add all money spent on goods and services (C + I + G + (X-M)).
3. Production/Output Method: Add value of all goods and services produced.

-Key Terms:

-1. GDP (Gross Domestic Product)
Total value of all goods and services made inside the country in a year.
Example: All goods and services produced in India in 2025.

-2. NDP (Net Domestic Product)
GDP minus wear and tear of machines and buildings.
Formula: NDP = GDP – Depreciation

-3. GNP (Gross National Product)
All goods and services produced by the country’s people, including what they make abroad.
Formula: GNP = GDP + Income from Indians abroad
Example: Money earned by Indian companies working in USA counts in India’s GNP.

-4. NNP (Net National Product)
GNP minus wear and tear.
Formula: NNP = GNP – Depreciation
Example: Shows the actual net income of Indians.

-5. PCI (Per Capita Income)
Average income per person.
Formula: PCI = National Income ÷ Population
Example: If India’s NNP is ₹200 lakh crore and population is 140 crore, each person gets ₹1.43 lakh.

-6. GVA (Gross Value Added)
Value of goods and services made in a sector after removing the cost of raw materials.
Example: A bakery sells cakes worth ₹50,000 in a month.
It spends ₹30,000 on flour, sugar, and eggs.
GVA = 50,000 – 30,000 = ₹20,000
The bakery created ₹20,000 of value through baking and selling.

-7. Green GDP
GDP after subtracting damage to environment (like pollution, deforestation).
Example: India produces goods worth ₹100 lakh crore but environmental damage costs ₹5 lakh crore → Green GDP = ₹95 lakh crore.

-Summary Table:
• GDP: Value of all goods/services inside country.
• NDP: GDP – wear and tear.
• GNP: GDP + money Indians earn abroad.
• NNP: GNP – wear and tear.
• PCI: Average income per person.
• GVA: Value added by a sector/industry.
• Green GDP: GDP after environmental damage.

6. Trade Cycles-Phases


-Meaning:
Trade Cycle (Business Cycle) is the regular rise and fall of economic activity in a country.
It shows how the economy grows and slows down over time.

-Key Features:
1. It is periodic – happens again and again.
2. It affects production, employment, and income.
3. Consists of alternating periods of growth and decline.

-Phases of Trade Cycle:

-1. Expansion / Boom:
• Economy grows, production increases.
• Employment rises, income and profits increase.
• High demand for goods and services.
Example: Factories produce more cars and people get jobs.

-2. Peak / Prosperity:
• Economy reaches its highest point.
• Output, income, and employment are at maximum.
• Prices may rise (inflation may start).
Example: Everything is selling fast, companies make huge profits.

-3. Recession / Slowdown:
• Economic activity starts declining.
• Production and sales decrease.
• Unemployment starts rising.
Example: Fewer cars are sold, some workers are laid off.

-4. Trough / Depression:
• Economy reaches lowest point.
• Very low production, income, and employment.
• Low demand for goods and services.
Example: Factories shut down, people lose jobs, spending is very low.

-5. Recovery / Revival:
• Economy starts growing again.
• Production and employment increase.
• Income rises, demand picks up.
Example: New businesses open, people start buying goods again.

-Summary Table:
Phase → Key Feature → Example
1. Expansion → Growth ↑ → Factories produce more
2. Peak → Maximum output → Companies earn highest profit
3. Recession → Decline starts → Sales fall, unemployment rises
4. Trough → Lowest point → Factories closed, spending low
5. Recovery → Growth resumes → Economy starts improving

-In short: Trade cycle = Boom → Peak → Recession → Trough → Recovery → Boom (and repeat).

Module II
Indian Economy
1. Salient features of Indian Economy and Structural changes


-Meaning:
Indian economy refers to the economic system of India, including production, consumption, distribution of goods and services, and its growth over time.

-Salient Features of Indian Economy:
1. Developing Economy: India is still developing with improvements in income, health, and education.
2. Mixed Economy: Co-existence of public (government) and private sectors.
3. Large Population: India has a huge population which provides labor but also increases unemployment.
4. Agriculture-based Economy: Agriculture employs a large part of population, though contribution to GDP is declining.
5. Low per Capita Income: Average income per person is low compared to developed countries.
6. Poverty and Unemployment: Large proportion of population below poverty line; unemployment is significant.
7. Dual Economy: Coexistence of modern (industrial) sector and traditional (agriculture) sector.
8. Service Sector Growth: IT, banking, and other services are growing rapidly.
9. Inflation and Price Instability: Prices of essential commodities fluctuate.
10. Infrastructure Deficiency: Roads, electricity, transport still need improvement.

-Structural Changes in Indian Economy:
Structural changes refer to shifts in the contribution of different sectors (agriculture, industry, services) to the economy over time.

- 1.Sectoral Shift:
• Agriculture → Industry → Services.
• Agriculture’s share in GDP is declining, Services are increasing.
Example: Share of agriculture in GDP ~18%, services ~55% (2025 approx.).

-2.Demographic Change:
• Population is increasing, with a young workforce.
• Urbanization is rising – people moving from villages to cities.

-3.Occupational Structure:
• More people moving from agriculture to industry and services.
• Example: IT and manufacturing sectors are employing more youth.

-4.Regional Imbalance:
• Some states are more industrialized than others.
• E.g., Maharashtra, Gujarat have more industries; Bihar, UP are more agriculture-based.

-5.Technological Changes:
• Modernization of agriculture and industries.
• Growth of digital economy and IT sector.

-In short:
• India is a developing, mixed economy with large population.
• Agriculture is declining, services are growing.
• Structural changes show shift from agriculture → industry → services.
• Urbanization, technology, and workforce changes are reshaping the economy.

2. Causes of Low Agricultural Productivity and Government measures to improve its productivity


-Meaning of Agricultural Productivity
Agricultural productivity means the amount of crop produced from a piece of land. If more output is produced from the same land, productivity is high. If less output is produced, productivity is low.

-Causes of Low Agricultural Productivity

-1. Small and Fragmented Land Holdings
Farmers have very small and scattered land, which makes use of modern machines difficult.

2. Dependence on Monsoon
Agriculture mainly depends on rainfall. Irregular rain reduces production.

3. Lack of Irrigation Facilities
Many areas do not have proper irrigation systems.

4. Traditional Methods of Farming
Use of old tools and outdated techniques lowers productivity.

5. Lack of Quality Seeds and Fertilizers
Poor quality inputs result in low yield.

6. Lack of Credit Facilities
Farmers do not get easy loans to buy modern equipment.

7. Poor Infrastructure
Lack of storage, transport, and market facilities causes wastage.

-Government Measures to Improve Agricultural Productivity

-1. Land Reforms
Government introduced land reforms to reduce inequality and consolidate land holdings.

2. Green Revolution
Introduction of HYV seeds, fertilizers, irrigation, and modern technology increased production.

3. Development of Irrigation Projects
Construction of dams, canals, and tube wells for water supply.

4. Subsidies
Subsidies on seeds, fertilizers, electricity, and irrigation facilities.

5. Agricultural Credit
Banks and cooperatives provide loans at low interest rates.

6. Minimum Support Price (MSP)
Government fixes minimum price to protect farmers from low market prices.

-7. Crop Insurance
Insurance schemes protect farmers from natural calamities.

-Conclusion
Low productivity is due to small land, lack of irrigation, traditional methods, and poor infrastructure. Government measures like land reforms, Green Revolution, subsidies, MSP, and irrigation help in improving agricultural productivity.

3. Poverty- Poverty Line, Causes and its alleviation strategies


-Meaning of Poverty
Poverty means a situation where a person is unable to fulfill basic needs like food, clothing, shelter, education and health.

-Poverty Line
Poverty line is the minimum level of income required to satisfy basic needs of life. People earning below this level are called poor. It is used by the government to measure poverty and to provide benefits to needy people.

-Causes of Poverty

1. Unemployment
Lack of job opportunities leads to low income and poverty.

2. Rapid Population Growth
High population increases pressure on resources and reduces income per person.

3. Low Agricultural Productivity
Poor farming methods and small land holdings reduce farmers’ income.

4. Unequal Distribution of Income
Wealth is concentrated in the hands of few people, leaving many poor.

5. Lack of Education and Skills
Illiteracy and lack of training reduce employment opportunities.

6. Inflation
Rise in prices reduces purchasing power of people.

7. Indebtedness
Heavy debts force poor people to remain in poverty cycle.

8. Social and Economic Backwardness
Caste system, discrimination and inequality also cause poverty.

-Poverty Alleviation Strategies

-1. Employment Generation Programs
Government creates job opportunities through rural and urban employment schemes.

2. Promotion of Education
Free and compulsory education improves skills and income opportunities.

3. Agricultural Development
Improving irrigation, seeds and technology increases farmers’ income.

4. Self-Employment Schemes
Providing loans and training to start small businesses.

5. Public Distribution System (PDS)
Providing food grains and essential goods at low prices to poor families.

6. Social Security Programs
Pension schemes, insurance and health programs for weaker sections.

7. Skill Development Programs
Training programs to improve employability of youth.

-Conclusion
Poverty is a serious economic and social problem. It is caused by unemployment, low income, population growth and inequality. Government uses various strategies like employment schemes, education, agricultural development and social security programs to reduce poverty and improve standard of living.

4. Population- Size and composition, Causes of high growth and Demographic Dividend


-Meaning of Population
Population means the total number of people living in a particular country or area at a given time.

-Size of Population
Population size refers to the total number of people in a country. A large population provides a big labour force but also creates pressure on resources like food, land, education and health facilities.

-Composition of Population
Population composition means the structure of population. It includes:

-1. Age Composition
It shows the number of people in different age groups:
• Children (0–14 years)
• Working age (15–59 years)
• Old age (60 years and above)

2. Sex Composition
It shows the number of males and females in the population (sex ratio).

3. Occupational Composition
It shows the number of people engaged in primary (agriculture), secondary (industry) and tertiary (services) sectors.

-Causes of High Population Growth

-1. High Birth Rate
Early marriages, lack of education and social customs increase birth rate.

2. Decline in Death Rate
Improvement in medical facilities, sanitation and nutrition reduces death rate.

3. Illiteracy
Lack of education leads to low awareness about family planning.

4. Poverty
Poor families consider children as earning members and social security.

5. Lack of Family Planning
Limited use of contraceptive methods increases population growth.

-Demographic Dividend
Demographic dividend refers to the economic benefit arising when the proportion of working-age population is higher than dependent population (children and old people).

-Advantages of Demographic Dividend

-1. Large Labour Force
More working people increase production and economic growth.

2. Higher Savings
Working population saves more, leading to higher investment.

3. Economic Development
More skilled youth can boost innovation and productivity.

-Conclusion
Population size and composition play an important role in economic development. High population growth is mainly due to high birth rate and low death rate. If properly managed through education, skill development and employment opportunities, demographic dividend can become a great advantage for economic growth.

5. NITI Aayog- Structure and Functions


-Meaning of NITI Aayog
NITI Aayog means National Institution for Transforming India. It is a policy think tank of the Government of India which gives advice on economic and development matters. It was established in 2015 replacing the Planning Commission.

-Structure of NITI Aayog

-1. Chairperson
The Prime Minister of India is the Chairperson of NITI Aayog.

2. Governing Council
It includes Chief Ministers of all States and Lieutenant Governors of Union Territories.

3. Vice-Chairperson
Appointed by the Prime Minister. He/She looks after day-to-day functioning.

4. Full-Time Members
Experts and specialists appointed by the Government.

5. Part-Time Members
Experts from universities and research institutions.

6. Chief Executive Officer (CEO)
Appointed by the Prime Minister for administration and coordination.

7. Special Invitees
Experts and specialists invited when required.

-Functions of NITI Aayog

-1. Policy Formulation
It provides strategic and technical advice to the Government on economic and social policies.

2. Cooperative Federalism
It promotes cooperation between Central and State Governments.

3. Long-Term Planning
It prepares long-term development plans and strategies for national growth.

4. Monitoring and Evaluation
It monitors government programs and suggests improvements.

5. Promoting Innovation
It encourages innovation, entrepreneurship and use of technology.

6. Sustainable Development
It works towards achieving sustainable and inclusive development.

-Conclusion
NITI Aayog is an important policy-making body of India. It focuses on cooperative federalism, strategic planning and sustainable development to transform India into a developed nation.

6. Food Security and recent trends


-Meaning of Food Security
Food security means that all people at all times have physical and economic access to sufficient, safe and nutritious food to live a healthy and active life.

-Elements of Food Security

-1. Availability
Sufficient quantity of food must be produced and supplied in the country.

2. Accessibility
People must have enough income to buy food at reasonable prices.

3. Affordability
Food should be available at prices that poor people can afford.

4. Stability
Food supply should remain stable without sudden shortages due to drought, flood or other disasters.

-Government Measures for Food Security

-1. Public Distribution System (PDS)
Provides food grains to poor families at subsidized prices.

2. Minimum Support Price (MSP)
Government purchases food grains from farmers at fixed minimum prices.

3. Buffer Stock
Government maintains stock of food grains to meet emergency needs.

4. National Food Security Act (NFSA), 2013
Provides legal right to subsidized food grains to eligible households.

-Recent Trends in Food Security

-1. Increase in Food Grain Production
Use of modern technology and improved seeds has increased production.

2. Digitalization of PDS
Use of technology like ration cards linked with Aadhaar to reduce corruption.

3. Focus on Nutrition
Government programs now focus on balanced diet and reduction of malnutrition.

4. Direct Benefit Transfer (DBT)
Subsidies are directly transferred to beneficiaries’ bank accounts.

5. Sustainable Agriculture
More emphasis on organic farming and climate-resilient crops.

-Conclusion
Food security is essential for economic and social development. Through PDS, MSP, buffer stocks and legal provisions like NFSA, the government ensures food availability and accessibility. Recent trends show improvement in production, digital systems and focus on nutrition and sustainability.

7. New Industrial Policy,1991


-Introduction
The New Industrial Policy, 1991 was introduced by the Government of India to reform the industrial sector. It aimed to remove restrictions, encourage private sector participation and integrate the Indian economy with the world economy. It is also known as LPG Policy (Liberalization, Privatization and Globalization).

-Objectives of New Industrial Policy, 1991

-1. To Increase Industrial Growth
To improve efficiency and productivity in industries.

2. To Remove License System
To abolish unnecessary industrial licensing and reduce government control.

3. To Encourage Private Sector
To give more freedom to private enterprises.

4. To Attract Foreign Investment
To invite foreign companies and capital into India.

-Main Features of New Industrial Policy, 1991

-1. Abolition of Industrial Licensing
Most industries were freed from compulsory licensing except a few related to security and environment.

2. Reduction in Public Sector Role
Number of industries reserved for public sector was reduced. Private sector participation was allowed.

3. Disinvestment
Government started selling shares of public sector enterprises to private investors.

4. Liberalization of Foreign Direct Investment (FDI)
Automatic approval for foreign investment in many industries.

5. Removal of MRTP Act Restrictions
Large companies were freed from restrictions under MRTP Act to expand their business.

6. De-reservation of Small Scale Industries
Many products were removed from exclusive small-scale sector list.

-Impact of New Industrial Policy

-1. Growth of Private Sector
Private companies expanded rapidly.

2. Increase in Foreign Investment
FDI inflow increased after 1991.

3. Increase in Competition
Competition improved quality and efficiency.

4. Economic Growth
Indian economy became more open and market-oriented.

-Conclusion
The New Industrial Policy, 1991 brought major economic reforms in India. It reduced government control, promoted private sector growth and opened the economy to global competition. It played an important role in transforming India’s industrial and economic structure.

8. Micro, Small and Medium Enterprises (MSMEs) – Problems and Policies


-Meaning of MSMEs
Micro, Small and Medium Enterprises (MSMEs) are small business units engaged in manufacturing, production and services. They require less capital investment and generate large employment opportunities. MSMEs play an important role in economic development and industrial growth.

Examples of MSMEs

-1. Micro Enterprises
• Small grocery shop
• Tailoring shop
• Beauty parlour
• Small dairy unit
• Handmade candle or soap making unit

-Classification of MSMEs
MSMEs are classified on the basis of investment and annual turnover into:
1. Micro Enterprises – Very small investment and turnover.
2. Small Enterprises – Moderate level of investment and turnover.
3. Medium Enterprises – Higher investment compared to micro and small but smaller than large industries.

-Problems of MSMEs

-1. Lack of Finance
MSMEs face difficulty in getting loans from banks and financial institutions.

2. Lack of Technology
Many units use outdated machinery and production methods.

3. Marketing Problems
They face strong competition from large industries and foreign companies.

4. Shortage of Raw Materials
Irregular supply and high cost of raw materials affect production.

5. Lack of Skilled Labour
Limited access to trained and skilled workers reduces efficiency.

6. Poor Infrastructure
Inadequate power supply, transport and storage facilities create difficulties.

7. Delayed Payments
Delay in receiving payments from buyers affects working capital.

-Policies and Measures for MSMEs

-1. Easy Credit Facilities
Government provides collateral-free loans and credit guarantee schemes.

2. Subsidies and Incentives
Subsidies on technology upgradation, exports and capital investment.

3. Skill Development Programs
Training programs to improve technical and managerial skills.

4. Marketing Support
Government helps MSMEs through trade fairs, exhibitions and e-marketing platforms.

5. Infrastructure Development
Establishment of industrial estates and special economic zones.

6. Protection Against Delayed Payments
Legal provisions to ensure timely payment to MSMEs.

7. Promotion of Digitalization
Encouragement for use of digital platforms and online business methods.

-Conclusion
MSMEs are the backbone of the economy as they generate employment and promote balanced regional development. Though they face problems like lack of finance, technology and marketing support, government policies and schemes aim to strengthen and promote their growth.

Financial Markets and Fiscal System
1. Indian Money Market- Features and recent trends


-Meaning
The Indian money market is a place where people and institutions borrow and lend money for a short period (less than 1 year). It helps businesses and banks manage their short-term needs.

-Key Participants
• Reserve Bank of India (RBI) – controls the money supply
• Commercial Banks – lend and borrow money
• Corporates – raise short-term funds
• Mutual Funds – invest in money market instruments
• Money Lenders – in unorganized sector

Examples of Money Market Instruments
• Treasury Bills (T-Bills) – Short-term government securities.
Example: 91-days t-bill

• Commercial Paper (CP) – Short-term loan issued by companies.
Example: A company issues a 3-month CP worth ₹50 lakh to raise funds.

• Certificates of Deposit (CD) – Banks issue them for short-term deposits.
Example: A bank issues a 6-month CD to investors.

• Call Money – Very short-term borrowing between banks (1 day to 14 days).

• Commercial Bills – Bills of exchange used to get short-term credit.
Example: A trader sells goods and gets payment through a bill.

-Features of Indian Money Market
1. Deals in short-term funds (less than 1 year).
2. Highly liquid instruments (easy to convert into cash).
3. Regulated by RBI.
4. Exists in organized (banks) and unorganized sectors (money lenders).
5. Safe investments (T-Bills are very safe).
6. Wholesale market – mostly large amounts are traded.

-Recent Trends with Examples
1. Digitalization – Banks now trade T-Bills and CPs online.
2. Growth of CPs and CDs – Companies like Reliance or Tata use CPs to raise short-term money.
3. Stronger RBI Role – RBI adjusts liquidity using repo rate and reverse repo rate.
4. Less unorganized sector – People now rely on banks instead of local money lenders.
5. Integration with Global Market – Indian banks trade short-term funds internationally.

-Conclusion
The Indian money market provides short-term finance to banks, companies, and the government. Instruments like T-Bills, CPs, and CDs make it easier to manage money efficiently. Digitalization and RBI regulation have made it safer and more modern.

2. Indian Capital Market- Features and Growth


-Meaning of Capital Market
The capital market is a place where long-term funds (more than 1 year) are raised by companies and governments. It helps in investment in shares, bonds, and other securities.

-Participants in Capital Market
• Companies – raise funds by issuing shares or debentures
• Investors – buy shares, bonds, or mutual funds
• Stock Exchanges – like NSE and BSE where securities are traded
• Financial Institutions – like LIC, UTI, and banks

Examples of Capital Market Instruments
• Shares/Equity – Buying a part of a company, e.g., buying Reliance shares.
• Bonds/Debentures – Loan to company or government, e.g., 10-year government bonds.
• Mutual Funds – Investors pool money and invest in shares and bonds.

-Features of Indian Capital Market
1. Long-term Funds – Money is borrowed or invested for more than 1 year.
2. Organized and Regulated – Regulated by SEBI (Securities and Exchange Board of India).
3. High Risk and High Return – Shares may give profit (dividends) but can also lose value.
4. Mobilization of Savings – Helps people invest savings in productive sectors.
5. Two Segments – • Primary Market: Company issues new shares (example: IPO of Paytm)
• Secondary Market: Buying and selling existing shares (example: trading Paytm shares on NSE)

-Growth of Indian Capital Market
1. Increase in Number of Companies – Many companies now list their shares on BSE/NSE.
2. Rise in Investor Participation – More people are investing through mutual funds and online trading.
3. Technological Advancements – Online trading, digital payment, and demat accounts have increased ease.
4. Regulatory Improvements – SEBI ensures transparency and protects investors.
5. Diversification of Instruments – New instruments like ETFs, derivatives, and corporate bonds are available.

-Conclusion
The Indian capital market helps companies raise long-term funds and provides investment opportunities for people. With SEBI regulation, technology, and more investor participation, the capital market in India is growing rapidly. Examples include trading shares on NSE/BSE and investing in mutual funds.

3. Measures of Money Supply in India


-Meaning of Money Supply
Money supply is the total amount of money available in an economy at a particular time. It includes currency, coins, deposits, and other liquid assets.

-Importance of Measuring Money Supply
• Helps control inflation and deflation
• Helps the government and RBI in monetary policy
• Indicates the liquidity in the economy

-Measures of Money Supply (as per RBI)
RBI classifies money supply into different categories called M1, M2, M3, and M4 (some books use M0, M1, M2, M3):

-1. M1 (Narrow Money)
Includes:
• Currency notes and coins held by public
• Demand deposits in banks (savings + current accounts)
• Other deposits that can be used for payments
Example: ₹5000 cash + ₹2000 in savings account = M1 = ₹7000

-2. M2
M1 + Savings deposits with post office savings banks
Example: M1 = ₹7000 + Post office savings ₹3000 → M2 = ₹10000

-3. M3 (Broad Money)
M1 + Time deposits with banks (fixed deposits)
Example: M1 = ₹7000 + Fixed deposits ₹5000 → M3 = ₹12000

-4. M4
M3 + Total deposits with post office savings institutions (all time and savings deposits)
Example: M3 = ₹12000 + Post office fixed deposits ₹2000 → M4 = ₹14000

- Simple Trick to Remember
M1 → cash + bank (use anytime)
M2 → M1 + post office savings
M3 → M1 + bank FD
M4 → everything (bank + post office all deposits)
-Conclusion
Measuring money supply helps RBI control inflation, interest rates, and economic growth. M1 shows liquid money available for daily transactions, while M3 and M4 include savings and deposits, representing total money in the economy.

4. Indian Tax Structure- Direct and Indirect Taxes


-Meaning of Tax
A tax is a compulsory payment made by individuals and businesses to the government to finance public goods and services.

-Indian Tax Structure
The taxes in India are mainly classified into Direct Taxes and Indirect Taxes.

-1. Direct Taxes
Meaning: Taxes paid directly to the government by the person on whom it is imposed.
Examples: Income Tax, Corporate Tax, Wealth Tax, Property Tax.

-Features of Direct Taxes:
• Paid directly by the taxpayer
• Cannot be shifted to others
• Progressive in nature (higher income pays more)

-2. Indirect Taxes
Meaning: Taxes collected by one person but paid to the government by another. Usually added to price of goods/services.
Examples: Goods and Services Tax (GST), Customs Duty, Excise Duty, VAT.

-Features of Indirect Taxes:
• Can be shifted from one person to another (like seller to buyer)
• Usually included in the price of goods/services
• Regressive in nature (affects everyone equally)

-Conclusion
The Indian tax system has both direct and indirect taxes. Direct taxes help reduce inequality, while indirect taxes are an important source of revenue. The combination ensures the government can fund development and public welfare programs.

5. Sources of Public Revenue


-Meaning of Public Revenue
Public revenue is the income earned by the government to meet its expenses on public services, development projects, and welfare schemes.

-Sources of Public Revenue
Government revenue comes from two main sources: Tax Revenue and Non-Tax Revenue.

-1. Tax Revenue
Taxes are compulsory payments made to the government.
Examples:
• Direct Taxes: Paid directly by individuals or businesses (Income Tax, Corporate Tax, Property Tax)
• Indirect Taxes: Paid indirectly through goods and services (GST, Customs Duty, Excise Duty)

-2. Non-Tax Revenue
Revenue earned by the government from sources other than taxes.
Examples:
• Fees and fines (Passport fee, Traffic fines)
• Dividends from public sector enterprises (like LIC, ONGC)
• Interest on loans given by government
• Profit from government-owned companies

-Other Sources:
• Grants and aids from foreign governments or organizations
• Sale of government assets (land, buildings)
• Public borrowing (loans, bonds) – though technically borrowed money, counted as revenue for spending

-Conclusion
The government raises revenue through taxes and non-tax sources to provide public goods, fund infrastructure, and run welfare programs. A balanced mix of tax and non-tax revenue ensures sustainable government finances.

6. Public Expenditure- Classification and Causes of growth of Public Expenditure


-Meaning of Public Expenditure
Public expenditure is the total spending done by the government to provide public goods, services, and welfare schemes for the citizens.

-Classification of Public Expenditure
Public expenditure can be classified in different ways:

-1. Classification by Purpose:
• Administrative Expenditure: Spending on government administration and salaries. Example: Salaries of government employees.
• Social Services Expenditure: Spending on education, health, and social welfare. Example: Mid-day meal scheme, public hospitals.
• Economic Services Expenditure: Spending on infrastructure, agriculture, and industry. Example: Roads, irrigation projects.
• Defense Expenditure: Spending on army, navy, air force, and security.

-2. Classification by Nature:
• Revenue Expenditure: Regular spending for daily running of government and payment of salaries. Example: Pension payments, subsidies.
• Capital Expenditure: Spending on assets or projects that create long-term benefits. Example: Construction of dams, schools, factories.

-Causes of Growth of Public Expenditure
Public expenditure is growing due to several reasons:

-1. Economic Development: Government spends more on infrastructure, education, and industry to promote growth.

2. Population Growth: More people require more health, education, and welfare services.

3. Inflation: Rising prices increase government spending on salaries, subsidies, and goods.

4. Expansion of Government Activities: Government now undertakes more functions like social security, environmental protection, and public health.

5. National Defense and Security: Increased expenditure on army, police, and internal security.

6. Welfare Programs: Social welfare schemes like MGNREGA, pension schemes, and housing require more funds.

-Conclusion
Public expenditure is an essential tool for economic growth and welfare. Its growth is caused by population increase, inflation, expanded government activities, and development programs. Proper planning ensures that public spending benefits the economy and society effectively.

7. Intergovernmental Fiscal Relations in India- Centre- State Fiscal Relationship and Finance Commission


-Meaning:
It is the system of sharing money and financial powers between the Central Government and State Governments. The goal is to make sure both Centre and States have enough money for their responsibilities.

-1. Centre-State Fiscal Relationship
-a) Tax Revenue Sharing:
Some taxes are collected by the Centre, some by States, and some are shared.
Example: Income Tax is collected by the Centre but shared with States. GST is collected and divided between Centre and States.

-b) Grants-in-Aid:
Centre gives extra funds to States for special programs.
Example: The Centre gives money to States for building hospitals or schools.

-c) Borrowing Powers:
States can borrow money but need permission from the Centre for external loans.
Example: A State wants to borrow from the World Bank for a highway project.

-d) Financial Autonomy:
States can levy some taxes themselves.
Example: Stamp Duty, Excise on alcohol, and Vehicle Tax.

-2. Finance Commission
-Meaning: A special body that decides how tax revenue should be shared between Centre and States.

-Constitutional Provision: Article 280 of Indian Constitution.

-Composition:
1 Chairman + 4 Members appointed by President.

-Functions:
1. Decide what share of taxes goes to States.
2. Suggest grants-in-aid for poorer States.
3. Advise on financial stability and reduce fiscal imbalance.

-Easy Example:
Suppose the Centre collects ₹1000 crore in taxes. Finance Commission may decide ₹300 crore goes to States and ₹700 crore stays with Centre.
Some States may also get extra grants for education or health programs.

-Importance:
Ensures States have enough money for local development.
Reduces inequality between rich and poor States.
Helps Centre and States work together financially.

-Conclusion:
Centre-State fiscal relations and Finance Commission ensure fair sharing of money and financial cooperation between Centre and States. This helps in balanced economic development across India.

1. Structural changes in India’s foreign trade since1991


-Introduction:
After 1991, India adopted the -Liberalization, Privatization, and Globalization (LPG) policy. This brought major changes in foreign trade, making it more open, diversified, and globally competitive.

-Structural Changes:
-1. Shift from Agriculture to Manufactured Goods:
Before 1991, India exported mainly agricultural products like rice, spices, and tea.
After 1991, manufactured goods such as textiles, automobiles, and engineering goods became major exports.
Example: Export of software services and textiles increased sharply.

-2. Diversification of Trading Partners:
Earlier, India traded mostly with USA, UK, and some European countries.
Now, India trades with ASEAN countries, China, Japan, Middle East, and Africa.
Example: China became a major import source for electronics and machinery.

-3. Increase in Export of Services:
Service sector exports, especially IT and software, have grown rapidly.
Example: Infosys and TCS exporting IT services worldwide.

-4. Reduction of Trade Barriers:
Import duties, quotas, and restrictions were reduced after 1991.
Easier imports of capital goods and technology improved industrial competitiveness.

-5. Growth in Foreign Direct Investment (FDI):
More FDI inflows improved export capacity and technology.
Example: Foreign companies investing in Indian auto and electronics industries.

-6. Shift from Import Substitution to Export Promotion:
Earlier focus was on producing goods locally to reduce imports.
Now the focus is on promoting exports to earn foreign exchange.
Example: Exportoriented textile and software parks.

-7. Emergence of Global Value Chains:
India became part of global production networks where parts are imported, assembled, and exported.
Example: Electronics and auto components are imported, assembled in India, and exported.

-Conclusion:
Since 1991, India’s foreign trade has undergone structural changes: diversification of products and trading partners, growth of services, export promotion, and integration into the global economy. These changes have increased India’s participation in world trade and foreign exchange earnings.

2. Balance of Payments Structure and Disequilibrium


-Meaning of Balance of Payments (BoP)
Balance of Payments is a record of all economic transactions between one country and the rest of the world during a specific period (usually one year).
It shows how much money is coming into the country and how much is going out.

Example:
If a country exports goods, money comes into the country.
If a country imports goods, money goes out of the country.
All these transactions are recorded in the Balance of Payments.

-Structure of Balance of Payments
BoP has two main accounts:

-1. Current Account
It records transactions related to goods, services, income, and transfers.

-a) Visible Items (Goods)
 These are physical goods like machines, clothes, oil, food grains, etc.
Export of goods brings money in.
Import of goods takes money out.

-b) Invisible Items (Services)
 These include services like banking, insurance, tourism, transport, IT services, etc.
Example: When a country provides IT services to another country, it earns foreign exchange.

-c) Income
 Includes interest, profit, and dividends received from abroad or paid to other countries.

-d) Transfers
 Money sent by workers living abroad to their families in their home country (remittances).

-2. Capital Account
It records transactions related to investment and loans.

-a) Foreign Direct Investment (FDI)
 When a foreign company invests in the country by opening a factory or business.

-b) Foreign Portfolio Investment (FPI)
 When foreigners invest in shares and bonds.

-c) Loans and Borrowings
 Loans taken from foreign countries or international institutions.

-Balance of Payments Always Balances
BoP always balances in accounting sense because deficit in one account is adjusted by surplus in another or by using foreign exchange reserves.

-Disequilibrium in Balance of Payments
Disequilibrium means imbalance between receipts and payments.
It happens when a country’s payments are more than its receipts or vice versa.

-Types of Disequilibrium

-1. Temporary Disequilibrium
Short-term imbalance due to seasonal changes, crop failure, sudden price rise, etc.

-2. Cyclical Disequilibrium
Occurs due to business cycles (boom and recession).

-3. Structural Disequilibrium
Happens due to structural changes in the economy.

-4. Fundamental Disequilibrium
Long-term and continuous deficit in BoP showing serious economic problems.

-Causes of Disequilibrium
Excessive imports
Low exports
High inflation
Political instability
Global economic slowdown

-Measures to Correct Disequilibrium

-1. Monetary Measures
Devaluation of currency
Deflation (control inflation)

-2. Non-Monetary Measures
Import restrictions (quotas, tariffs)
Promoting exports
Encouraging foreign investment
Import substitution (produce goods within the country)

-Conclusion
Balance of Payments shows the economic relationship between a country and the rest of the world. A stable BoP is important for economic growth.

4. Commercial trade policy


-Meaning of Commercial Trade Policy
Commercial trade policy refers to the policy adopted by a government to regulate foreign trade (imports and exports) of a country.
It includes rules, regulations, and measures to control trade with other countries.

-Objectives of Commercial Trade Policy
To protect domestic industries.
To promote exports.
To reduce imports.
To maintain balance of payments.
To earn foreign exchange.

-Types of Commercial Trade Policy

-1. Free Trade Policy
Free trade means there are no restrictions on imports and exports.
There are no or very low tariffs and quotas.

Example:
Countries freely buy and sell goods without heavy taxes or restrictions.

-Advantages of Free Trade
Consumers get goods at lower prices.
More variety of goods.
Encourages healthy competition.

-2. Protection Policy (Protectionism)
Protection policy means the government protects domestic industries from foreign competition.

-Methods of Protection
Tariff – Tax on imported goods.
Quota – Limit on quantity of imports.
Subsidy – Financial help to domestic producers.
Import licensing – Permission required to import goods.

Example:
If the government puts high tax on imported cars, people will buy locally produced cars.

-Instruments of Commercial Trade Policy
Tariffs
Quotas
Subsidies
Export incentives
Import restrictions

-Importance of Commercial Trade Policy
Helps in economic development.
Protects new and small industries (infant industries).
Maintains balance of payments.
Creates employment opportunities.

-Conclusion
Commercial trade policy helps a country control its foreign trade. It can follow free trade or protection policy depending on its economic condition and goals.
